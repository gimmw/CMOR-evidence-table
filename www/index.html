What matters to you when choosing treatments for knee osteoarthritis?

There are many treatment options available for the management of knee osteoarthritis.

Some of them are highly effective, reducing pain and improving joint function and overall quality of life; others have limited or unclear effect on pain or disability. Some are widely available, easily accessible, and affordable for all people with osteoarthritis; others are expensive or involve long travel or waiting times to receive treatment. Some are very safe, with few or no side-effects and little risk of harm; others may have potentially severe complications.

Understanding how consumers, healthcare providers, health policy makers, and other stakeholders weigh up and trade off these different attributes of treatments is crucial to ensure good value, effective, accessible, acceptable, and equitable care can be made available to all people with osteoarthritis.

This application presents the results of a study designed to elicit from key stakeholders the attributes of treatment considered most important in making decisions about osteoarthritis care and their preferences for trade-offs among these attributes; to describe the performance of osteoarthritis interventions against the attributes, informed by high-quality clinical practice guidelines and systematic reviews of peer-reviewed research; and to prioritise interventions by assessing their performance relative to stakeholder or individual preferences.

The application is interactive – you can filter the interventions to be included in the analysis, alter the preference weights used to evaluate trade-offs between attributes, or select evidence specific to early-, mid-, or late-stage disease using the drop-down options menus, and switch between viewing the preference ranking plot and the underlying evidence table.

To get started, click the button on the plot above.

Cost-effectiveness of osteoarthritis adjunctive management options

Cost-effectiveness is a crucial criterion for any treatments funded by the New Zealand public health system.

This application presents the results of a cost-effectiveness study of adjuctive osteoarthritis treatments, as recommended by the Royal Australian College of General Practitioners' Guideline for the management of knee and hip osteoarthritis (2nd edition).

The results are based on a computer simulation model we developed that generates a hypothetical cohort of individuals based on the NZ population distribution and runs them through a sequence of annual health state transitions, such as onset of knee osteoarthritis, progression of the disease, health loss due to osteoarthritis, and provision of treatments for their condition.

We use the model to estimate the cost-effectiveness of treatments by comparing the accumulated lifetime health outcomes (in quality-adjusted life years, or QALYs) and healthcare costs in a situation in which the treatment is made available to individuals meeting eligibility criteria to one in which it is not.

In this analysis, all individuals with osteoarthritis were eligible for recommended 'core' treatment (patient education, exercise therapy, and weight management), and we evaluated the adjunctive interventions by adding them to the model (one at a time) for patients whose osteoarthritis was not adequately controlled by core treatment only.

What the figure shows

The figure reports the lifetime per-capita incremental costs (from the NZ health sector perspective) and health-related quality of life gains (measured in quality-adjusted life years, or QALYs), for each recommended adjunctive treatment option.

For comparison, it also reports the stakeholder preference score (from the previous tab), and allows you to generate an overall intervention ranking by adjusting the relative weight put on each of these criteria.

Points to the right (larger health gains) and bottom (lower costs) of the figure represent more cost-effective treatments. The size of the label represents the overall intervention ranking. By default, this score is based on cost-effectiveness only—this can be adjusted using the slider above the table to the right of the figure.

Click on the help icon above the figure for more help on the functionality of the app.

CMOR

The Center for Musculoskeletal Outcomes Research is a multidisciplinary community of researchers, clinicians, policy-makers, and educators dedicated to improving parient health outcomes. We conduct and disseminate clinical trials and health services and health economic research addressing decision-making and outcomes for patients with musculoskeletal disorders.

CMOR is a research centre at the University of Otago, located within the Department of Surgical Sciences, Dunedin School of Medicine, and closely affiliated with clinical services of the Southern District Health Board.

MOA

The Management of Osteoarthritis research programme investigates the management of osteoarthritis at the interface between primary care and secondary care, with an emphasis on the contribution of non-pharmacological, non-surgical interventions, primarily exercise physiotherapy and manual physiotherapy.

The MOA programme of research incorporates a range of research methodologies, centred around health technology assessment (HTA). HTA is a multidisciplinary field that examines the health and economic implications of medical interventions, bridging clinical epidemiology, medical decision-making and health economics, and health services and policy decisions.

How it works

In this application, interventions for the clinical management of knee osteoarthritis are characterised according to eight attributes: recommendation (appropriateness for the stage of the disease), quality of evidence, cost, duration of effect, accessibility, risk of mild/moderate harm, risk of serious harm, and effectiveness. Each attribute describes between 3 and 5 levels of performance, in increasing order of desirability (for the details of the levels described by each attribute, click the help icon next to the attribute heading in the Preference Weights panel).


Options:

Intervention performance :

The performance scoring for each intervention can differ at different stages of the disease course (for example, total joint replacement has little evidence and is generally not recommended/appropriate for early-stage disease, but is strongly recommended for patients with severe late-stage disease); to incorporate this in the ranking model, evidence tables can be constructed for early, mid, or late-stage disease using the 'Timing of interventions' option.

In future iterations of the evidence table, we will also incorporate the option to select different sources of evidence to inform the table and resulting preference scores. (At this stage, only the Royal Australian College of General Practitioners' Guideline for the management of knee and hip osteoarthritis, 2nd edition is available.)

Filter :

A total of 74 interventions are included in the current version of the evidence table. These can be filtered by category and, if required, by the selection of specific interventions in the Interventions option panel.

Preference Weights :

Interventions are ranked by summing up their total "preference weights", or values associated with the performance of the intervention on each of the attributes, using weights calculated from the derived preferences of key stakeholders in our study. These can be changed if desired, using the sliders/numeric inputs in the Preference Weights panel, to investigate the resulting effects on the intervetion ranking.


Outputs:

Outputs can be shown as either a plot of the preference weightings for each intervention, or an evidence table reporting the performance of each intervention across the eight attributes.

To switch between the preference plot and viewing the underlying evidence table, click the toggle switch to the left.

The plot shows the aggregate preference score for each selected intervention, and the contribution of each attribute level to this total. You can click on the bar for an intervention to provide a summary of the attribute levels and associated preference weights for that intervention. Click elsewhere on the plot to remove the summary.

The evidence table reports the performance of each selected intervention on the eight attributes, according to the specified evidence source and timing of interventions, and the resulting aggregate preference score using the specified preference weights. Interventions are (by default) ranked by total preference score. The table can be sorted by intervention name, attribute, or preference score by clicking on the column headers, and searched for specific interventions or attribute levels using the search box.

Further details of the study, including recruitment of key stakeholders, theoretical basis for the preference-elicitation study, and construction of the evidence tables, can be found here.

Timing of Interventions

Some interventions are considered more appropriate or have higher quality evidence at different stages of the disease course. In this study, we considered the appropriateness of each intervention for early/mild, mid/moderate, and late/severe osteoarthritis, corresponding approximately to their recommendation as first-line (core long-term management), second-line (optional adjunctive management), or third-line (surgical management for end-stage osteoarthritis) treatment options.


Options:
  • Early/Mid/Late: the recommendation attribute is adjusted based on whether interventions were considered as core, optional adjunctive, or end-stage surgical management treatment options
  • Overall: the evidence table is based on considering each intervention at their 'most recommended' timing

Further details of the adjustments for timing of interventions will be provided in upcoming publications.

Preference Weights

The attributes are aggregated, by default, using the New Zealand stakeholder preference weights elicited as part of our project The impact and management of rising osteoarthritis burden.

If you want to use alternative preference weights, these can be set using the sliders or numeric input boxes in this section.

All preference weights must sum to 100. This is done automatically when calculating values for the table and plot; to see the re-calculated values in the input fields, use the 'Normalize Weights' button.

Weights can be reset to the NZ stakeholder weights using the 'Reset' button

Recommendation for using the treatment at the given stage of OA

Providing or using the right treatments or services at the early/mild, mid, or late/advanced stage of OA. For example, it would not be recommended or appropriate to use powerful drug treatments such as opioids before, say, self-management and education, physical exercise, or less-powerful drug therapies such as paracetamol.


Levels (best to worst):
  • Strong for: all or almost all informed people would use the treatment at this stage
  • Conditional for: most informed people would use the treatment at this stage, but not all
  • Neutral
  • Conditional against: most informed people would try another treatment at this stage, but not all
  • Strong against: all or almost all informed people would try another treatment at this stage

Note: ...but not all means that a substantial number of people would still choose (or not choose) the treatment

Quality of the evidence – how confident you are that the treatment works

The extent to which one can be confident that the effects of the treatment or service described are real.

'Evidence' can mean different things to different people, however, there is an accepted hierarchy of how valid each source is. For example, anecdotal claims about the effectiveness of treatment in advertisements, from peers or family members, or from individual treatment providers may not be as convincing as independent health professional advice, rigorous research, peer-reviewed Systematic Reviews, or authoritative Clinical Practice Guidelines.


Levels (best to worst):
  • High: further research is very unlikely to change our confidence in the likely effect of the intervention
  • Moderate: further research is likely to have an important impact on our confidence in the likelihood of effect of the intervention and may change the estimate
  • Low: further research is very likely to have an important impact on our confidence in the likelihood of effect of the intervention and is likely to change the estimate
  • Very low: Any estimate of the treatment effect is very uncertain

Cost of Treatment

Total financial costs relevant to the use or provision of health care for OA - e.g. costs to the health system, out-of-pocket costs to the consumer, and the societal costs of providing health care for OA. Societal costs include lost wages and tax revenue due to time away from work, reduced employment, or early retirement.


Levels (best to worst):
  • Low: $0-$100 per month OR less than $1500 total
  • Medium: $100-$1000 per month OR $1500-$15,000 total
  • High: $1000 or more per month OR $15,000+ total

Duration – how long the treatment effect lasts

The length of time the benefits of the treatment have been shown to last - e.g. the beneficial effects of surgery, if appropriate, may last for 10-15 years after initial healing has occurred, with little ongoing care until 10-15 years have elapsed. In contrast, many drug therapies have only been shown to be effective for several months from initiation of treatment.


Levels (best to worst):
  • Long: the effects of the treatment are experienced for longer than one year
  • Medium: the effects of the treatment are experienced for several months to one year
  • Short-Medium: the effects of the treatment are experienced for up to several months
  • Short: the effects of the treatment are experienced for up to 4-6 hours

Accessibility

The extent to which the treatment or service can be accessed by people with OA. For example, the distance to nearest provider, wait time, and the ability for people from diverse cultural and linguistic groups or sociodemographic backgrounds to equally access health care for OA (fairness).


Levels (best to worst):
  • Accessible: the treatment can be accessed by the person living with OA in a week or so, regardless of their travel needs
  • Neither accessible nor inaccessible
  • Inaccessible: There may be a waiting time of a month or more to receive the treatment; the provider may be inconvenient to reach; or the treatment may not be accessible at all because of health system-related factors

Risk of mild or moderate side-effects

Mild to moderate treatment side-effects associated with comfort or safety - e.g. temporary pain, discomfort, nausea, heartburn or stomach pain.


Levels (best to worst):
  • Low: 1 in 4 chance (25%)
  • Moderate: 2 in 4 chance (50%)
  • High: 3 in 4 chance (75%)

Risk of serious harm

Severe treatment side-effects - e.g. implant failure, drug toxicity, stomach bleeding or ulcer.


Levels (best to worst):
  • Low: 1 in 500 chance (0.2%)
  • Moderate: 1 in 200 chance (0.5%)
  • High: 1 in 50 chance (2%)

Effectiveness

The ability for the treatment or service to achieve the desired result - e.g. the change in pain and function caused by the intervention.

Effectiveness is different to quality of the evidence because it describes the impact, or how ‘big’ the change caused by the treatment is, not how likely it is to happen, or how confident you are that it’ll happen – this is the 'quality of the evidence'. For example, a highly effective treatment with a very low quality of evidence means that the likelihood, or chance of it actually working is very small but, if it did work, it would have a high/large impact on pain and/or function.

Effectiveness is measured by the standardised mean difference (SMD) in pain levels after treatment, defined as the mean reduction in pain levels following treatment divided by the standard deviation of baseline (pre-treatment) pain levels.


Levels (best to worst):
  • High: SMD > 0.5
  • Medium: 0.2 < SMD < 0.5
  • Low: SMD < 0.2

Willingness-to-pay

Whether or not a treatment is considered to be cost-effective for the public health system depends on how much we are willing to pay, as a society, to improve health outcomes.

Common willingness-to-pay thresholds considered relevant for health policy making are between one and three times the country's gross domestic product (GDP) per capita for each year of healthy life gained.

This slider allows you to switch between willingness-to-pay levels of one ($52,300), two ($104,600), and three ($156,900) times GDP per capita.

Health-related quality of life measure

Evaluating cost-effectiveness requires a method of assigning health-related quality-of-life (HRQoL) values to individuals' health states.

Our model uses the SF-6D HRQoL instrument, a widely-used measure of overall health status based on the domains of pain, physical functioning, role limitations due to health problems, energy/vitality, mental health and social functioning.

As a sensitivity analysis (since some clinical trials do not collect general health measures like the SF-6D), we have also estimated HRQoL using the osteoarthritis disease-specific WOMAC pain scale.
